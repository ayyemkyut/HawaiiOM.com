Workers' Compensation FAQ

Your Questions, Answered

Navigating a workers' comp claim can feel overwhelming. Here are clear, plain-language answers to the questions injured workers ask most.

Yes. Under Hawaii law (HRS §386-27), workers' compensation operates under an "open panel" system. This means you have the right to choose your own treating physician — you are not limited to a doctor selected by your employer or insurance carrier. You can choose Hawaii Occupational Medicine as your treating physician for your work injury.

No. When your visit is authorized under a workers' compensation claim, we bill your WC insurance carrier directly. There are no copays, deductibles, or out-of-pocket costs for you. We work with HEMIC, FICOH, and all major Hawaii WC carriers.

An IME is a medical evaluation performed by a doctor who has not been involved in your treatment. It's usually requested by the insurance carrier, your employer, or sometimes your attorney to get an independent opinion about your injury, diagnosis, or treatment. The IME doctor reviews your records, examines you, and writes a report with their medical opinion.

An IME is not treatment — it's an evaluation. You won't receive a bill for it (the requesting party pays), and you should answer the doctor's questions honestly and completely. You have the right to bring someone with you to the exam for support.

MMI means your condition has stabilized and is unlikely to improve significantly with further treatment. It doesn't necessarily mean you're 100% healed — it means your condition is as good as it's expected to get. Once your doctor determines you've reached MMI, your WC case may move toward a permanent impairment rating (if applicable) and case closure.

Reaching MMI does not mean your benefits automatically stop. It's a clinical milestone, not the end of your rights.

An impairment rating is a medical assessment that assigns a percentage to any permanent loss of function you have as a result of your injury. Dr. Harris uses the AMA Guides (the standard reference) to determine this rating. The percentage matters because it directly affects the amount of permanent partial disability (PPD) benefits you may be entitled to receive.

A higher rating generally means higher benefits. The rating is based on objective medical findings, not on how you feel day-to-day, which is why having an experienced occupational medicine specialist perform the rating is important.

Please bring:

  • Your workers' compensation claim number (if you have one)
  • A photo ID (driver's license or state ID)
  • A list of your current medications
  • Any imaging results or medical records related to your injury
  • A written description of how the injury happened — include the date, what you were doing, and how it occurred

If you don't have all of this, that's okay — come anyway and we'll work with what you have.

Hawaii law (HRS §386-142) prohibits employers from retaliating against employees for filing a workers' comp claim. This includes firing, demotion, or any other adverse action taken because you exercised your right to file a claim. If you believe you've been retaliated against, you may want to consult with an attorney who specializes in workers' compensation law.

If your WC claim is denied or a treatment request is refused, you have the right to appeal. Hawaii's Department of Labor and Industrial Relations (DLIR) Disability Compensation Division handles disputes. You can request a hearing, and many injured workers choose to work with a workers' comp attorney at this stage. Dr. Harris can provide detailed medical documentation to support your case if it goes to dispute.

A Fitness for Duty exam determines whether you're medically able to safely perform your job. Your employer may request one before you return to work after an injury or medical leave. The goal is to make sure you're safe — both for yourself and your coworkers. If you have restrictions, Dr. Harris will clearly document them so your employer can accommodate you appropriately.

Dr. Harris is fluent in both English and Japanese. If Japanese is your preferred language, your entire evaluation, exam, and follow-up discussion can be conducted in Japanese. 日本語での診察が可能です。ご安心ください。

What's in an IME Report

Every IME report is a comprehensive, self-contained medical-legal document. Here's what you can expect to receive:

Detailed History — Mechanism of injury, symptom progression, prior medical history, occupational history, and the claimant's subjective report.
Physical Examination Findings — Documented objective findings from a thorough clinical examination.
Records Reviewed — Complete list of all medical records, imaging, and documents reviewed for the case.
Causation Analysis — Medical opinion on whether the condition is work-related, with clinical reasoning and supporting references.
Diagnosis & Treatment Recommendations — Current diagnosis, appropriateness of past treatment, and recommendations for future care.
MMI Determination — Whether the claimant has reached Maximum Medical Improvement, with rationale.
Impairment Rating — When applicable, a whole-person impairment rating using AMA Guides with detailed calculations.
Work Restrictions & Functional Capacity — Current functional limitations and specific work restrictions, if any.
Answers to Specific Questions — Direct responses to any questions posed by the referring party in the engagement letter.

Report Standards

Every report is written to withstand cross-examination. Opinions are evidence-based, internally consistent, and supported by cited medical literature where applicable.

Supplemental Reports

If additional records become available after the initial report, supplemental opinions can be provided upon request.
